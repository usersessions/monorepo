# The Hailuo Visual DNA Playbook
### Reverse-Engineering High-Converting Ad Aesthetics into Pure Visual Prompt Engineering

**Core constraint respected throughout:** every prompt is a single continuous ~10s shot, zero dialogue, zero on-screen text/UI requests, zero lip-sync — pure camera + material + physics + light, tuned to what Hailuo/MiniMax actually renders well (fabric drag, hair layering, liquid dynamics, kinetic momentum).

## 1. Big Tech (Apple)

**The Visual Hook (0–3s):** The trigger isn't the product — it's the *reveal mechanic*. A single raking light sweeps across an extreme macro material surface in near-darkness, and the eye is forced to wait for context. The brain can't classify the object yet, so it can't scroll away. The payoff is a slow, physically-grounded materialization (light → form → full product) rather than a hard cut.

**The Hailuo Lexicon:**
| Cinematic | Material/Texture | Motion/Physics |
|---|---|---|
| probe lens dolly-out | brushed titanium | sweep |
| macro rack focus | frosted glass | glide |
| 180° orbit reveal | matte ceramic | levitate |
| low-angle hero shot | anodized chamfer edge | settle |

**The Prompt Formula:**
`[Near-black void] + [Extreme macro material detail] + [Single directional light sweep] + [Camera dolly/orbit pulling back] + [Full product reveal, hero angle, ambient falloff]`

**God-Tier Prompt 1:**
"Extreme macro shot in near-total darkness: the brushed titanium edge of a device fills the frame, grain visible. A single cool-white beam sweeps slowly left to right across the surface, catching every micro-groove in a thin bright line. As the light passes, the camera performs a slow probe lens dolly-out combined with a gentle 90-degree rotation, pulling back to reveal the full device levitating in a seamless charcoal-gradient void. The chamfered edge glints once as it settles into a low-angle hero frame. Light fades to a soft ambient glow. No hands, no reflections of a room, no text."

**God-Tier Prompt 2:**

No hands, no packaging, no on-screen text."

### Quick-Reference: Universal Cross-Category Rules for the Pipeline
- **Never** request: on-screen text, UI elements, subtitles, logos rendering legibly, lip-sync, or dialogue.
- **Always** anchor the prompt in one continuous camera move (dolly, orbit, tracking, or locked-off) — avoid multi-shot/cut language, since Hailuo renders one continuous take.
- **Front-load** the physics verb (sweep, explode, ripple, squeeze, cascade) — MiniMax's real-world physics strength is the actual differentiator, so it should never be an afterthought in the prompt.
- **Lighting direction** should always be stated explicitly (backlit, raking, volumetric) — ambiguous lighting produces flat, generic results.

# PHASE 2: 10 Pure-Visual Pattern Interrupts

Here are 10 pure-visual pattern interrupts, each built around a physics mechanic Hailuo actually renders well — so they're not just "creative ideas," they're mechanically buildable.

**1. The Impossible Levitation**
Product hangs motionless mid-air with no visible support, slowly rotating, while dust or liquid droplets around it obey gravity normally. The contradiction (one object ignoring physics everyone else obeys) is what stops the thumb.

**2. The Freeze-and-Reverse Splash**
Liquid or powder mid-splash, frozen at peak dispersion, then visibly reverses — droplets pulling backward into the container. Unnatural motion direction is one of the strongest interrupt triggers there is.

**3. The Burial and Unearthing**
Product buried in sand, gravel, or powder. A sudden burst/sweep clears the material away in one motion, revealing the object underneath. Reads as "discovery," not "advertisement."

**4. The Shatter Reveal**
An outer shell (ice, chocolate, plaster, glass) cracks and falls away in real time to expose the product inside. Destruction-as-reveal is inherently satisfying and impossible to scroll past mid-crack.

**5. The Macro Misdirect**
Open on a texture so close-up it's unidentifiable (could be skin, fabric, stone, liquid — ambiguous). Camera racks focus and pulls back in one continuous move to resolve into the full product. The brain can't classify it, so it waits.

**6. The Magnetic Snap**
Two halves of a product (case, cap, clasp) fly together and lock with a sudden magnetic-snap motion — fast, decisive, mechanical. Works especially well for anything with a closing mechanism.

**7. The Cross-Section Cutaway**
Camera appears to pass *through* the product's outer shell into a visible internal layer (like an X-ray push-in), revealing structure or ingredients that are normally hidden. Curiosity gap resolved visually, with zero narration needed.

**8. The Collision Reveal**
Two unrelated objects collide (a fist into flour, a ball into liquid, a weight dropped onto sand) and the impact cloud clears to reveal the product sitting at the point of impact, undisturbed. The mismatch between violent motion and pristine product is the hook.

**9. The Color-Shift Under Light**
Product surface appears one color/texture, then a light sweep or angle change causes it to visibly shift (iridescent, color-changing, or matte-to-glossy transition). Reads as "this shouldn't be possible," which holds attention involuntarily.

**10. The Gravity-Defying Pour**
Liquid or granules poured *upward* into the product, or a pour that halts and hovers mid-stream before resuming. Inverting one familiar physical process (pouring) is a cheap, high-impact interrupt because everyone's visual expectation is that specific and that strong.

**Common thread for your pipeline:** every one of these is interrupt-via-physics-violation-or-ambiguity, not interrupt-via-cutting or interrupt-via-speed. That matters for Hailuo specifically since it's a continuous-shot, single-take model — these all work *within* one unbroken shot rather than needing a hard cut, which is where MiniMax is strongest and where most generic UGC-style prompts (that assume multi-cut editing) actually fail to render well.

# PHASE 4: 10 Pattern Interrupts for E-Commerce

Because AI video engines like MiniMax (Hailuo) can't rely on a creator shouting "Stop scrolling!", you have to hack the viewer's brain using pure physics, lighting, and motion.
The human eye is biologically wired to track sudden movement, stark contrast, and textural changes. Here are 10 pure visual "pattern interrupt" hooks designed specifically for AI video generation, guaranteed to stop the scroll in the first 0–3 seconds without a single word spoken.

1. The Extreme Macro Smear (Skincare & Cosmetics)
* The Hook: A polished metallic spatula aggressively pressing into a thick, luxurious dollop of cream, smearing it across a pristine glass surface.
* Why it works: It triggers a tactile, ASMR-like response. The brain instantly wants to feel the texture.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Extreme macro probe lens, thick viscous texture, metallic reflection, smooth kinetic drag, high-contrast lighting.

2. The Zero-Gravity Collision (Food, Beverage, Supplements)
* The Hook: The product falls in ultra-slow motion through a void, colliding mid-air with a splash of liquid (water, coffee, or bright juice) or exploding ingredients (citrus slices, berries).
* Why it works: It creates a "frozen in time" spectacle that is impossible to see with the naked eye.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Ultra-slow motion, 1000fps phantom camera, fluid dynamics, mid-air collision, liquid droplet physics, suspended in zero gravity.

3. The Sudden Silhouette Flare (Tech, Gadgets, Automotive)
* The Hook: The video starts pitch black for a fraction of a second. Suddenly, a razor-sharp, volumetric beam of light rapidly sweeps across the scene, outlining the sleek metallic edge of the product.
* Why it works: The darkness causes the pupil to dilate, making the sudden flash of light highly arresting and dramatic.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Pitch-black environment, sweeping laser volumetric lighting, rim light reveal, anodized metal texture, high dynamic range.

4. The Kinetic Dust Shockwave (Sneakers, Durables, Hardware)
* The Hook: The product sits half-buried in vibrant neon powder or fine dark sand. Suddenly, a slow-motion invisible shockwave hits, violently blowing the powder away in a perfect ring to reveal the item.
* Why it works: MiniMax excels at particle physics. The chaotic but beautiful dispersal of dust creates an instant visual "wow" factor.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Particle physics, slow-motion dust shockwave, kinetic blast, matte texture, high-speed macro, suspended airborne particles.

5. The Satisfying Compression (Shoes, Mattresses, Activewear)
* The Hook: A massive, transparent glass cylinder slowly and heavily presses down onto the product, squishing its soft foam or fabric, which immediately bounces back into perfect shape.
* Why it works: It proves product quality visually while satisfying the brain's love for elasticity and deformation (think hydraulic press videos).
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Elastic deformation, heavy glass weight, compression physics, macro fabric weave, kinetic rebound, clean studio lighting.

6. The Submerged Ripple (Watches, Jewelry, Waterproof Goods)
* The Hook: The product is dropped perfectly flat into a pool of deep, dark, viscous liquid. The camera sits exactly at the water line, capturing the slow, perfect ripples expanding outward.
* Why it works: Water and liquid dynamics are notoriously hard to film practically, but mesmerizing when done right. It communicates premium durability.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Water-line split shot, slow-motion surface tension, dark viscous liquid, perfectly symmetrical ripples, submerged refraction.

7. The Whip-Pan Micro Focus (Apparel, Bags, Any physical product)
* The Hook: The camera starts 10 feet away, showing a blurry background. In less than a second, it aggressively whips forward (dolly-in), stopping dead on a microscopic view of the product's stitching or texture.
* Why it works: The extreme speed transition physically pulls the viewer into the screen, snapping their attention to focus.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Aggressive whip-pan, hyper-speed dolly-in, sudden rack focus, microscopic macro, deep depth of field, fabric threading.

8. The Impervious Shatter (Luggage, Phone Cases, Outerwear)
* The Hook: A sheet of thin ice or fragile glass falls directly onto the product, instantly shattering into a million shimmering pieces that rain down around the completely undamaged item.
* Why it works: It’s a classic demonstration of strength, but the visual of shattering glass creates chaotic, beautiful light reflections that command attention.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Shattering glass physics, slow-motion debris, crystalline light refractions, impact momentum, indestructible, hard-shell texture.

9. The Fabric Wind-Tunnel (Clothing, Fashion, Bedding)
* The Hook: The product is on a pedestal. Suddenly, massive sheets of translucent, brightly colored silk blow violently across the frame, elegantly wrapping and draping around the product.
* Why it works: MiniMax has some of the best fabric rendering in the world. The chaotic, flowing motion of cloth is incredibly elegant and visually hypnotic.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Violent wind physics, translucent silk draping, flowing fabric dynamics, elegant kinetic drag, soft studio backlight.

10. The Instant Thermal/Color Shift (Supplements, Energy Drinks, Gym Gear)
* The Hook: The product sits in a freezing cold, icy blue environment with condensation dripping down it. In a flash, the lighting aggressively snaps to a burning neon crimson, and the condensation turns to steam.
* Why it works: A total palette swap in the first two seconds shocks the brain's color receptors. It communicates a rapid transition in energy or state.
* MiniMax Trigger Words: Instant lighting shift, cold cyan to aggressive neon crimson, condensation physics, rising vapor steam, cinematic temperature change.
